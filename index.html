Interactive Chess
I created this chess board after finishing a command-line version of it and deciding that I wanted to actually see the board. The logic was originally written in Ruby, but I used a tool called Opal to convert my code into Javascript. From there it was just a matter of adding some html/css. You can check out its repository over on my github.

Feel free to play around with the board. Piece rules have been implemented, meaning that rooks can only go in perpindicular directions while bishops are restricted to diagonals. Kings have a "reach" of 1, while queens are unlimited. Additionally, you can not make moves that put your king in check and you can not leave your king in check. As of now, there is no computer AI to play against, but I'm considering it for a future project!

Just click a piece to select it, then click an allowed space to place it.

My Personal Webpage


Posted on March 15, 2016

So I'm glad to say that I've been pretty productive since I last posted! After finishing up with my Chess program, I started thinking about what my next project should be. I was pretty sure that I wanted to create my own, personal webpage/portfolio site, but it's hard to have a portfolio with nothing in it! My chess program was still fresh in my mind, and I considered going through the trouble of creating some sort of on-page terminal to access it (since it's a CLI program) but that seemed like less than an ideal solution. This all led me to consider creating an actual, visible, on-page chess game. I didn't really have a clear idea of how it would best be done, but I was fairly confident that all of the (what I assumed to be appropriate) tools were within my reach.

For instance, I knew that to do pretty much anything interactive in the browser I would have to use Javascript, which I'd dabbled (a miniscule amount) in before. I've been working in html/css a lot recently and I knew that I could accomplish the looks with them. That only left the logic, the brains. I already had those made, but not in the correct language. So that left me with two options. I could attempt to re-write the logic in a language (Javascript) that I didn't know, or I could try to find some way to use the Ruby code that I'd already written. I decided to look into the latter.

The first most obvious thought was to use Rails. I've worked with Rails a few times before, but not nearly enough to consider myself proficient. So, I decided that my most likely plan of action would be to learn more Rails and go from there. It wasn't the best plan, but I've always been the type to get a rough idea, attempt to execute and reevaluate if/when the time comes. First on the list was to learn some basic Javascript. I learned some of the most basic bits, tooled around on Codecademy and the like for a couple days before realizing that Stackoverflow was probably going to supply everything I needed for my purposes.

By this point, it was time to learn Rails. But another idea hit me; why not try to port the Ruby into Javascript? There are all sorts of JS frameworks/ports on the web; I don't even know Javascript and I know that! So, I googled for something along the lines of 'Ruby to Javascript compiler' and found Opal. Opal is exactly that: A Ruby to JS compiler. It takes your Ruby code and converts it into a (quite massive) JS file that you can then use like any other JS file. The reason it's so large is that Opal's task isn't as small as it may seem at first. It has to convert your code but it also has to convert the entire Ruby language and all of the files that entails too, minus some minor things that JS and Ruby share, like strings and arrays. So the resulting JS file is just over 20,000 lines of code not counting any other JS you might write for the run-of-the-mill type stuff. Which, by the way, is pretty intimidating for someone whose biggest repository on github has maybe 15 commits! But nervousness aside, I was quite happy that I'd found a way to circumvent what otherwise would have been quite a lot of work to get the 'brains' of my program connected to the 'face'.

Then there was just the matter of implementation. It took me a few caffeine-fueled days to get done and I learned some new things along the way. I learned that jQuery is nicer to use and look at than plain old JS. I learned that Opal has some pretty difficult to understand documentation, thought I'm still not sure if it's the documentation or the fact that I'm a noob at programming, haha. I became even more familiar with html/css. And all sorts of other things that I can't think of off the top of my head. Suffice to say, the whole process from the ding! of idea to the actual nitty-gritty of implementation is just a little less opaque now.

So, after wading through a smorgasbord of bugs, I had my "Interactive Chess Board." The final product wasn't perfect, the biggest issue being that there's a slight lag when you place a piece, no doubt caused by a combination of my spaghetti code and the enormous Opal/JS file. But it worked! and that was all that mattered to me. At this point, with Chess Board in hand, it was time to create my website! If you're reading this post you've no doubt had a chance to look at it. It's nothing special, but again, it gave me the chance to refine my html/css/js skills.

So, here we are! I've had blinders on for the past couple of weeks while I've toiled away on my projects, but now that I'm just taking them off and looking at the fruits of my labor, I'm pretty happy! It feels awesome to look at something and be able to say, "I did that!" I'm probably going to take a couple days off now, but I'm already trying to come up with an idea for the next project. Can't wait!

More Rspec and Flow Control!


Posted on March 17, 2015

Nothing too new today. I continued working through the Test First Ruby problems and finished all except for one single stinkin' Rspec test. It's on the twelfth problem which has you construct a Reverse Polish Notation calculator. You can read up on Reverse Polish Notation here. A quick and incomplete rundown of it is this; instead of framing a mathematical expression with the operands on either side of the operator like so, 2+2, you place your two operands then your operator like so, 22+. Crazy right? It gets crazier. You can have expressions that look like 235*/ which would evaluate in the same way as 2 / (3*5). Basically, each operator is used with the two terms before it. In this example, since 3 and 5 are the first two terms, the multiplication sign is grouped with them. Then since (3*5) can be considered one term and 2 is another, the division symbol is used for both of them. Like I said, it's pretty crazy though it does negate the need for parentheses, since the order of operations will always be obvious. Personally, I'd just as soon keep my parentheses thank-ya-very-much.

Anyway, I managed to create push, sum, minus, divide, times, and tokens methods for my RPNCalculator class. The implementation was fairly straightforward. The given specs had me make use of a stack, which in Ruby's case is just an array. So, I kept track of my stack which would push a given number, then when any of my operator methods were called I would check to see if @value == nil. If it did, I'd be sure to pop the last two numbers on the stack, operate on them, then set the return value = @value. And of course, if @value didn't equal nil, I would just pop the last value and operate between it and my @value instance variable. No biggie, but I didn't think ahead for other possible use cases. See, all of my specs except the last, were very simple use cases, which meant that my program didn't break. It wasn't until I began working on my evaluate method, that I realized I was in for it. The evaluate method is supposed to take a given string such as, "1 4 2 * + 3 9 / -" and RPNCalculate in order. So basically Calc.push(1), Calc.push(4) and so on, in order like that. The problem I ran into was that after I ran the first two operators, I would get a value, I would push the next two numbers to the stack, I'd run the next  operator and then my original value would be overwritten. Sure, I could hard code it to pass my final spec, but I've labored to make sure every other program was clean and concise, and I'll be damned if I give up on my last spec!

So I'm going to let it be for tonight, and I'll take another look at it tomorrow. I did start a new RPNCalculator from scratch to see if I could go about my flow control in a different and better way and I've got some neat ideas, but nothing working yet. Anyway, it's a neat problem and I'm glad I got to share it on my blog.

I've been looking at the things I want to get done before sending in my coursework and hitting the next Coding Test, and it looks like I'll be coming in right on my 10 day minimum time expenditure, which is cool. I'll probably send everything off and schedule the next test Saturday. How a mutation made me question reality


Posted on March 6, 2015

So, from what I've researched about the admissions process for AA, you really want to have your stuff down. Not only do you want to be able to complete the coding challenges, but you should be prepared to do it whiteboard style, in a timed setting. So, I've been running through the prep-work problems again today. I've gotten much faster and am very pleased with the progress I've made. Even though I was able to do them all yesterday, I still took a considerable amount of time on each problem. Due in part to the fact that I've done them already, and in part to me simply improving, my ability to logic my way through a problem has definitely improved. Plus, writing the problem down on paper definitely helps.

Anyway, I was working on problem thirteen which requires you to capitalize all of the words in a given string. To do it, you have to first, break the words of the string apart into individual array elements, then use the upcase method to capitalize the first letter of each array element. I was comparing my solution to the given solution and noticed something very strange. I'll see if I can finagle Wordpress' formatting and get it posted below:

def capitalize_words(string)
words = string.split(" ")

idx = 0
while idx < words.length
word = words[idx]

word[0] = word[0].upcase
idx += 1
end

return words.join(" ") end
Yeahhh, the indention's off, but hopefully you get the gist. Heh heh heh, erm. Anyway! Notice that the words array is never directly altered. Instead, it's altered through the string variable word. This was throwing me off big time, and when I ran it to make sure it worked and it did...Man, I started looking around the room to make sure I didn't see any deja-vu-black-matrix-cats running around because I was confused. I couldn't see how that code could possibly work. But, before I started delving through my medicine cabinet for some red pills, I decided to consult the helpful folks over at Stackoverflow. God, I love that place. You can ask the most obscure question, and someone will have gotten back to you within two seconds complete with sources and everything. But anyway, the phenomenon that I was experiencing is, evidently, referred to as mutation. The word string element is just a reference to the words array element. So, when you upcase the first letter of word it's actually effecting the words[idx] element. Crazy stuff, but I'm glad I stumbled across it now instead of halfway through the coding test.

Also, while I was studying the mutation phenomenon, I noticed a neat little thing over in the ruby doc. Apparently, you can use negative numbers to reference the end of a string. So, say you have string word = "imastring" and you were to say word[-4] = "b", then your result would be word = "imastbing". Not really all that useful now that I think about it, but it's neat nonetheless.

Anyhoo, I intend to keep on studying ruby for at least another couple of days. Perhaps longer, as I want to really have this stuff down before I take the coding test and if I'm so lucky as to make it far enough, the interview portion. Attending App Academy could potentially be life changing for me. Compared to self-teaching, anyway. Of course, I'm doing my best to manage my expectations. They only admit 3-5% of applicants, so there is the very real possibility that I won't get in.

The Odin Project


Posted on March 1, 2015

Hi! Welcome to my blog. My name's Morgan Martin. I'm a 20 year old guy with a huge interest in computers in general and web development in particular. I've always had an interest in computers, and growing up I was always my parents' solution to whatever tech problems arose in the house. Unfortunately, I've never taken the natural next step and taught myself to program, but that all changes now.

So, before I get into what my plan is, let me give a little more background on myself. After high school, I did go to college for computer science. Unfortunately, after three semesters I decided that the college life wasn't for me, in part mostly to growing amounts of debt that I didn't want to have to deal with after college. So, I dropped out and decided to self-teach myself the important parts. I've spent the past couple of years working jobs that aren't fulfilling such as waiting tables, or installing carpet. Just enough to put some food in my belly. I've decided however, that it's either now or never so I've decided to get on with it and start learning Web Development.

I'm choosing Web Development mostly because it's the hip new thing and because there are so many great resources to learn it online. Namely, The Odin Project. This is by far the best resource I've found in my years of dabbling with self-teaching programs online. I've already completed the Introduction to Web Development 101 track, which was mostly stuff I already knew. I've also worked through to the first project which requires you to create the Google homepage with your newly acquired HTML and CSS skills. It came out a little rough, and I had to borrow from some other's solutions, but it definitely pushed me to hone my skills a little more. You can see it on my Github page, here.

Oh! I would be remiss if I didn't give credit for my idea to blog my WebDev journey, all of which goes to Joshua Kemp over at Confessions of an Unlikely Developer. Long story short, he started out as a horseshoe-maker and after a grueling 9 months, he got a job as a Ruby WebDev. His story is truly inspirational and it's what gave me the idea to blog all of this, so you should definitely check him out if you're looking for some great advice.

This post is already getting pretty long, so let me sum things up really quick. Basically, I intend to do one assignment/project on TOP a day. I will also make one blog post a day detailing my progress. This process should take about 5-6 months. At that time, I'd like to start contributing to some open source projects and creating some of my own, too. Thanks for reading!
